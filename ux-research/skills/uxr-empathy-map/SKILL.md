## MANDATORY PREPARATION

Invoke `/dexlab` — it contains design principles, anti-generic rules, and the **Context Protocol**. Follow the protocol before proceeding.

## Steps

1. **Gather your source material** — Interview transcripts, observation notes, survey responses. Don't build an empathy map from assumptions or team brainstorms. No data = no map.

2. **Define the scenario** — Who is this user and what situation are we mapping? "Maria, 34, scheduling her first telehealth appointment." Specificity makes the map useful.

3. **Fill the four quadrants from evidence** —
   - **Says**: Direct quotes from research. No paraphrase. Use their words.
   - **Does**: Observable behaviors and actions. What you actually watched them do.
   - **Thinks**: Inferred thoughts — things they didn't say out loud but implied. Note confidence level.
   - **Feels**: Emotional states. Use feeling words: anxious, frustrated, relieved, skeptical. Not "they felt confused" — that's a think, not a feel.

4. **Identify tensions** — Where does what they SAY contradict what they DO? These gaps are the most valuable insights. ("I always read reviews" + skips reviews in testing.)

5. **Surface pains and gains** — Below the quadrants: what frustrates them? What do they hope for? These feed directly into opportunity framing.

6. **Validate with research data** — Every item on the map must be traceable to a data source. If you can't cite it, remove it.

## Anti-Generic Rule

An empathy map filled from a team brainstorm is a projection map, not an empathy map. It shows what the team assumes users feel, not what users actually experience.

## Next Steps

Use this empathy map to ground opportunity statements and design briefs. Don't let it sit alone — it's most useful when paired with synthesis and HMW ideation.
